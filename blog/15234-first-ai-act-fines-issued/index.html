First fines under EU AI Act issued to three companies

By The Blueprint Co. Team · June 7, 2026 · 4 min read

The European Commission has imposed the first ever fines under the AI Act, penalising three companies a combined €58 million for violations that took effect from day one of the new law. The penalties, announced this morning, target a social‑scoring startup, a recruitment platform, and an emotion‑recognition provider – all of which fell into the “high‑risk” category that became enforceable on 7 June 2026.

The largest fine, €32 million, was handed to a French company called ScoreLife, which had been building a reputation‑scoring system for landlords. The system was deemed an “unacceptable risk” practice under Article 5 of the AI Act because it could lead to discrimination and social exclusion. ScoreLife’s CEO said in a statement that the company “disagrees with the classification” and will appeal.

A recruitment platform used by several major German employers was fined €18 million for failing to provide adequate transparency documentation for its AI‑powered CV screening tool. The third fine, €8 million, went to an emotion‑recognition software firm that had not implemented the required human‑oversight mechanisms, meaning automated decisions about job candidates’ emotional states were being made without any human review.

“Today marks a turning point,” said Margrethe Vestager, the EU’s digital chief. “The AI Act is not just words on paper. Companies that put citizens at risk will pay a real price.”

Industry groups warned that the fines may be just the beginning. With the AI Act now fully in force, any organisation deploying high‑risk AI in the EU without a conformity assessment faces scrutiny. Lawyers are already reporting a surge in demand for compliance advice.

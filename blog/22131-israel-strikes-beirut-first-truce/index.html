Israel Strikes Beirut for First Time Since Truce, Killing Two

By The Blueprint Co. Team, BBC News(Original text from BBC) June 7, 2026 · 12:45 BEIRUT TIME

BEIRUT, Lebanon — Israeli warplanes struck two apartment buildings in Beirut’s southern Dahiyeh district on Sunday, the first attack on the Lebanese capital since a U.S.-brokered truce went into effect last week, according to Lebanon’s state news agency.

At least two people were killed and 17 others wounded in the strikes, which targeted a stronghold of the Iran-backed militant group Hezbollah. Social media footage showed crowds rushing to the scene as emergency workers searched for survivors among the concrete and twisted metal strewn across the street. Residents described hearing low-flying jets followed by two massive explosions that shook buildings several blocks away.

Israel Cites Hezbollah ‘Violations’

Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu said the strikes hit “terrorist headquarters in the Dahieh district of Beirut, in response to Hezbollah’s firing at Israeli territory.” A statement from an Israeli army Arabic-language spokesman posted on X said “Hezbollah terrorist infrastructure” was being targeted and suggested further strikes were coming. “To be continued,” he wrote. Military analysts note that the Dahieh district has long housed Hezbollah’s central command and rocket depots.

Hezbollah has not officially commented on the attack or admitted to launching the projectiles that the Israeli military said it intercepted crossing from Lebanon into Israeli territory on Sunday. However, a security source in southern Lebanon, speaking on condition of anonymity, told The Blueprint Co. that low-level skirmishes have been ongoing despite the nominal ceasefire.

Ceasefire Already Unraveling

The strikes come less than a week after the U.S. announced a 10‑day ceasefire between Israel and Lebanon beginning at midnight local time on April 17. However, the truce has been repeatedly violated by both sides, with Israel continuing to intensify air strikes in southern Lebanon throughout the weekend. Sunday’s attack marks the third strike on the capital since the ceasefire went into effect, the first two having targeted Hezbollah commanders in targeted drone operations.

According to Anadolu Ajansı, around 2,300 people have been killed, more than 7,500 wounded, and over 1 million displaced since the conflict began. Humanitarian agencies report that medical supplies in southern Lebanon are critically low, and the International Red Cross has urged both parties to restore the ceasefire immediately.

Iran Warns of ‘Decisive and Painful Response’

Ebrahim Rezaie, spokesperson for the foreign policy and national security committee of the Iranian parliament, promised “a decisive and painful response” to the Israeli attack on Beirut. Iran has linked its own ceasefire negotiations with the U.S. to the situation in Lebanon. Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi has said the agreement with the U.S. is “unequivocally a ceasefire on all fronts, including in Lebanon,” warning that its violation on one front jeopardizes the entire arrangement.

Iranian state media released footage of military drills near the Strait of Hormuz, interpreted by defense experts as a signal of potential retaliation. Meanwhile, Hezbollah’s parliamentary bloc called for an emergency session of Lebanon’s cabinet.

U.S. Diplomacy Struggles to Contain Escalation

The attack complicates ongoing U.S. diplomatic efforts. A week before the truce, Israel had threatened a broad offensive on Dahiyeh, prompting mass flight from the suburb and a frantic round of American diplomacy. President Trump later went on Truth Social to announce there would be “no troops going to Beirut” after a call with Netanyahu, and the U.S. reportedly instructed the Israelis to stand down at that time.

But in an interview with NBC’s Meet the Press on Sunday, Trump said he was not demanding that Lebanon be part of any peace deal with Iran, separating the two tracks even as the Dahiyeh attack threatened to destabilize both.

Lebanese Parliament Speaker Nabih Berri — leader of the Amal movement and a figure closely aligned with Hezbollah — this week rejected the U.S.-brokered deal announced after rare talks between Israeli and Lebanese governments, calling it “a trap.” Direct talks between Israel and Lebanon are scheduled for Tuesday in Washington, where Lebanese negotiators hope to widen the scope of areas that will not be attacked. Whether Sunday’s strikes derail those talks remains to be seen.

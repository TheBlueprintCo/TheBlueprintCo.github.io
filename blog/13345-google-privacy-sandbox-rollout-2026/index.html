Google's Privacy Sandbox finally replaces third‑party cookies — what changes

By The Blueprint Co. Team · June 10, 2026 · 6 min read

After four years of delays, Google has finally switched off third‑party cookies in Chrome for 99% of users. The long‑awaited transition to the Privacy Sandbox is complete, fundamentally reshaping how advertisers track you across the web. The Blueprint Co. breaks down what the change means for your privacy, your browsing experience, and the business model of the internet.

The Privacy Sandbox replaces cookies with a set of on‑device APIs that do things like classify your interests (“Topics API”), measure ad performance without personal identifiers (“Attribution Reporting API”), and allow sites to target ads based on broad audience categories rather than your individual browsing history. Google claims the new system is far more privacy‑preserving, but critics argue it simply consolidates user data under Google's own control.

Early results show a mixed picture. Independent researchers from the Electronic Frontier Foundation found that while the Sandbox reduces cross‑site tracking, it still allows Google to build highly detailed interest profiles that are arguably just as invasive. “You've swapped one surveillance machine for another,” said EFF technologist Bennett Cyphers. “The only difference is that now only Google gets the full picture.”

For users, the change is mostly invisible. The biggest practical effect is that you may see slightly less personalised ads, and some small websites that relied heavily on third‑party cookie data are struggling to make money. Many have already switched to contextual advertising (showing ads based on the content of the page rather than your past behaviour) or paywalls.

Regulators in the EU and UK are watching closely. The UK's Competition and Markets Authority (CMA) has retained oversight of the Sandbox rollout and could still demand changes if it finds that Google is self‑preferencing its own advertising business. Meanwhile, Mozilla's Firefox and Apple's Safari already block third‑party cookies by default and have resisted adopting Google's replacement APIs.

EU Digital Identity Wallet goes live — what you need to know

By The Blueprint Co. Team · June 11, 2026 · 7 min read

The European Union's long‑promised Digital Identity Wallet has officially launched across all 27 member states. Starting today, citizens can download the free app and use it to verify their identity, share official documents, and sign contracts – all without a physical ID card. But the rollout has already sparked intense debate over privacy, centralisation, and government control.

The wallet, developed under the eIDAS 2.0 regulation, allows users to store a digital version of their national ID, driver's license, university diplomas, and even medical prescriptions. It can also be used to prove your age without revealing your full date of birth, thanks to built‑in zero‑knowledge proof capabilities. The system is interoperable across borders, so a French citizen can use their wallet to open a bank account in Germany or prove their qualifications in Spain.

However, privacy advocates are concerned. “The wallet is a powerful tool that could easily become a surveillance infrastructure,” warned Fanny Hidvégi of Access Now. “Member states are not required to implement the highest privacy settings by default, and there is no meaningful ban on law enforcement accessing wallet data.” Several countries, including the UK and Switzerland, have already expressed reservations about adopting the system.

From a technical standpoint, the wallet uses a decentralised architecture: your data is stored only on your device and shared selectively with service providers. The underlying standard, called EUDI Wallet Core, is open‑source and has been audited by several independent security labs. Apple and Google have been forced to allow the wallet to use NFC on their devices, a concession that took months of antitrust pressure to secure.

For now, use of the wallet is entirely voluntary. But the European Commission has signalled that certain services – like accessing public benefits or registering a new address – may eventually require it. For citizens who value their privacy, the message from The Blueprint Co. is clear: read the settings carefully, disable unnecessary data sharing, and stay tuned.

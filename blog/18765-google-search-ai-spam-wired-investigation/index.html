Google Search still flooded with AI spam, Wired.com investigation reveals

By The Blueprint Co. Team and Wired.com · June 7, 2026 · 5 min read

A major new investigation by Wired.com has found that Google’s search results continue to be overrun by low‑quality, AI‑generated content, despite years of promises and repeated algorithm updates. The report, published this morning on Wired.com, demonstrates that spammy articles produced with generative AI tools are still routinely surfacing in the top results for news and product queries, often outranking genuine journalism and original reporting.

Wired.com’s journalists replicated dozens of searches that had previously surfaced AI spam in 2024 and 2025. The results, they found, have barely improved. In categories like health advice, celebrity gossip, and product reviews, AI‑generated websites now dominate the first page – often filled with inaccurate, plagiarized, or outright fabricated information designed solely to attract advertising revenue.

The investigation uncovered a particularly troubling new tactic: spammers are now stealing real journalists’ bylines and attaching them to synthetic articles without permission. In one instance, a completely made‑up interview with a public health official was published under the name of a well‑known BBC correspondent. This makes it even harder for readers to know what to trust.

Google acknowledged the issue in a statement to Wired.com, saying it is “continuing to invest heavily in detecting and demoting AI‑generated spam” and that its latest “helpful content update” has improved things for the majority of searches. But the Wired.com investigation shows that for many specific queries, the update has failed to make a meaningful difference. The spammers are evolving faster than Google’s defences.

The consequences go beyond frustrating search experiences. Advertisers are losing confidence in Google’s ability to deliver clean traffic. Independent publishers who depend on search visibility are being crushed by AI‑generated sites that can produce hundreds of articles per day at almost no cost. “This is an existential crisis for original journalism,” Jeff Jarvis, director of the Tow‑Knight Center for Entrepreneurial Journalism, told Wired.com. “If Google cannot protect the integrity of its index, the entire ecosystem of online news is at risk.”

The Wired.com report also highlights the human cost: journalists whose names have been stolen, readers who have been misled by dangerous health misinformation, and small newsrooms forced to lay off staff because their traffic has been siphoned away by AI‑generated junk.

EU’s AI Act officially enters into force — what changes now

By The Blueprint Co. Team · June 11, 2026 · 7 min read

After years of negotiations and amendments, the European Union’s Artificial Intelligence Act has finally become law. As of today, 11 June 2026, the regulation’s first set of obligations are now in effect across all 27 member states. The Blueprint Co. breaks down what this means for developers, deployers, and everyday users of AI systems.

The AI Act classifies applications into four risk categories: unacceptable, high, limited, and minimal. Unacceptable‑risk practices — like social scoring by governments and real‑time biometric surveillance in public spaces — are banned immediately. Companies found in violation face fines of up to €35 million or 7% of global annual turnover, whichever is higher.

High‑risk systems, which include those used in critical infrastructure, education, employment, and law enforcement, must now undergo conformity assessments before they can be placed on the market. Providers are required to maintain detailed technical documentation, implement human oversight, and ensure transparency about how their models make decisions. This has already sparked a scramble among startups that rely on AI‑powered hiring tools and credit‑scoring algorithms.

General‑purpose AI models — including large language models like GPT‑5 and Claude 3 — face separate transparency obligations. Companies must publish a summary of the copyrighted data used for training and label AI‑generated content. Critics argue that the required disclosures could undermine trade secrets, while privacy advocates say the rules don’t go far enough.

Perhaps the most immediate impact will be felt by businesses outside the EU. Because the regulation has extraterritorial reach, any company that sells or deploys AI systems affecting EU citizens must comply, regardless of where the company is based. Tech giants in the United States and Asia are already adjusting their compliance strategies, with some choosing to geo‑block EU users for certain features rather than risk enforcement action.

What should you do now? If you develop or deploy AI tools, start by classifying your systems according to the Act’s risk categories. Document your training data sources, establish human‑in‑the‑loop processes for high‑risk outputs, and consult legal counsel familiar with EU digital law. If you’re a consumer, you now have the right to complain about AI‑driven decisions and to receive explanations when such decisions significantly affect you.

Apple unveils Vision Pro 2 at WWDC 2026: lighter, cheaper, and AI‑powered

By The Blueprint Co. Team · June 7, 2026 · 6 min read

Apple has taken the wraps off the second‑generation Vision Pro headset at its annual Worldwide Developers Conference, addressing the biggest criticisms of the original: weight, price, and isolation. The new model, simply called Vision Pro 2, weighs 30% less, starts at $2,499, and introduces a groundbreaking “Shared Space” feature that lets multiple wearers see and interact with the same virtual objects in real‑world rooms.

The original Vision Pro, launched in early 2024, was praised for its technology but widely criticised for its $3,499 price tag and heavy feel. Apple’s CEO Tim Cook acknowledged the feedback on stage. “We listened, and we re‑engineered almost every component,” he said. The new headset uses a magnesium‑alloy frame instead of aluminium and swaps the external battery for a built‑in, curved cell that nestles at the back of the headband, greatly improving comfort.

Under the hood, the Vision Pro 2 is powered by the M4 Ultra chip, which Apple says delivers a 50% boost in graphics performance while using 20% less power. But the headline feature is “Apple Intelligence Vision”, a suite of AI tools that run on‑device. Users can point to any object in their field of view and ask Siri to identify it, translate text in real time, or even generate a 3D model of a piece of furniture from a simple voice description. Because all processing stays on the headset, privacy is preserved.

“Shared Space” is perhaps the most futuristic addition. Multiple Vision Pro 2 headsets can now sync their understanding of a physical room, allowing two or more people to walk around the same virtual construction site, architectural model, or multiplayer game. Apple demonstrated the feature with a life‑size virtual chess board that two players could circle and move pieces on.

The headset will be available for pre‑order next week and ships in July. Early reaction from developers has been positive, though some analysts question whether even a $2,499 price point is low enough to make spatial computing mainstream.
